My research interests include climate  variability and change, climate modeling, physical oceanography, air-sea interactions, biogeochemistry, and large-scale climate dynamics. 

My current graduate research is focused on the ‘signal-to-noise paradox’ in climate modeling--the idea that there is too much variability, or noise, among a model’s ensemble members, which causes the ensemble mean to have low variability and a weak signal in comparison to observations. This implies that climate models often underestimate or completely fail to capture real-world trends within the climate system, which can be associated with external forcing in addition to natural internal variability. My research aims to examine some of these observed trends that climate models have failed to capture and attempt to understand why they have been missed, potential impacts associated with these trends, and how they can be better modeled, using tools including multi-model large ensembles and high-resolution models.
